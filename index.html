About

Sea level rise is a major consequence of climate change, increasing the risk of flooding, erosion, and loss of coastal ecosystems. Developing effective adaptation strategies requires accurate projections of future sea level change, the accuracy of which depends on the length of the available time series.

Tidal measurements have been collected for centuries and represent some of the oldest environmental observations. However, much of this information remains stored in historical paper records and has not yet been digitised, limiting its accessibility and use in scientific analysis. As a result, many existing sea level datasets extend back only to the late nineteenth century despite the existence of earlier observations.

Data archaeology initiatives have demonstrated the value of recovering and digitising historical tide records. Examples include extended tide gauge records from Amsterdam (Netherlands), Brest (France), and Sheerness (UK). Nevertheless, many historical records remain undigitized and are at risk of deterioration or loss.

This website provides a catalogue of historical tide records worldwide. The aim is to improve the discoverability of these records, support data rescue initiatives, and provide information that can help prioritise and plan digitisation efforts. By facilitating access to these historical observations, the catalogue contributes to the preservation and extension of long-term sea level datasets for use in climate research and coastal planning.

Historical tidal observations were recorded in 2 primary formats: ledgers and marigrams (tide charts). Examples of both formats are shown below.

Inventory of Digitisation Tools

Contribute
